  • Poem

    Krishna Ki Chetavani — Ramdhari Singh ‘Dinkar’

    “Krishna Ki Chetavani” is among the most stirring poems in Hindi literature. On the eve of war, Krishna offers peace to Duryodhana and warns of the ruin that hubris invites. The poem’s force comes from its fusion of compassion and cosmic power—Krishna as guide and as a vast, moral order that insists on justice. Dinkar’s diction is musical yet razor-sharp; images swing from intimate counsel to universe-sized vision. Even stripped of context, the verses feel urgent and ethical: first persuasion, then the immovable demand of dharma. It is beautiful not only in sound and rhythm, but in how it frames courage—standing for the right thing when conciliation fails. The poem endures because it speaks to leaders and citizens alike: peace is precious, but justice cannot be bartered away.

  • Poem

    Madhushala — Harivansh Rai Bachchan

    A lyrical meditation on life, longing, and fellowship, “Madhushala” turns the tavern into a symbol of human connection and transcendence. Its cascading rhyme and refrain make it musical and memorable, while the imagery—cup, wine, and tavern—becomes a philosophy of openness and empathy. The poem invites readers to drink not from oblivion but from experience: to meet joy and sorrow without pretense, to dissolve rigid boundaries of caste, creed, and dogma, and to find kinship in the shared act of living. Bachchan’s voice is playful yet profound; he spins celebration into contemplation, and intimacy into universality. “Madhushala” endures because it welcomes everyone to the same table—urging us to taste art, friendship, and life itself with equal courage and tenderness.
