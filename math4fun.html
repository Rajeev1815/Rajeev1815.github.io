    Airplane Seating (Lost Boarding Pass)

    There are n passengers and n seats. Passenger 1 sits randomly; each later passenger takes their own seat if free, otherwise picks randomly. What‚Äôs the probability that the last passenger sits in their own seat? More generally, what‚Äôs the probability that the k-th passenger from the end finds their seat vacant?

    Added: Aug 30, 2025
    Show Solution ‚ñæ Hide Solution ‚ñ¥

    Recap

    P(A) = (number of outcomes where A happens) / (total number of outcomes). Here A is ‚Äúthe last passenger sits in the proper seat.‚Äù

    Key idea (two special seats)

    Track only seat 1 (for passenger 1) and seat n (for the last passenger). Passenger 1 chooses a seat. Anyone whose seat is taken picks a random free seat; think of a ‚Äúwrong-seat token‚Äù that jumps each time that happens. The process stops the first time the chain hits either seat 1 or seat n:

    • If the chain hits seat 1 first, seat n was never taken ‚áí the last passenger sits correctly.
    • If it hits seat n first, the last passenger‚Äôs seat is already occupied ‚áí they cannot sit correctly.

    Counting by how many sit wrong (k-view)

    Let k be the number of people who end up in the wrong seat. We never have k = 1. For each admissible k (0, 2, 3, ‚Ä¶, n) there are two symmetric ways the chain can close: at seat 1 or at seat n. Exactly half of those cases give the last passenger their correct seat.

    Result for the last passenger

    P(last sits correctly) = 1/2 for every n ‚â• 2. Symmetry between the two special seats makes them equally likely to be the first one hit.

    Generalization: k-th from last

    Consider passenger number n ‚àí k + 1 (the k-th from the end). Track the special set S = {1, n‚àík+1, n‚àík+2, ‚Ä¶, n} of size k+1. Just before that passenger boards, the wrong-seat token is equally likely to be on any seat in S. Their seat is free exactly when the token is on one of the other k seats. Hence: P(k-th from last finds own seat free) = k / (k + 1).

    Checks: k=1 ‚Üí 1/2 (the last passenger), k=2 ‚Üí 2/3, k=3 ‚Üí 3/4, independent of n (for n ‚â• k+1).

    Alternative intuition via cases

    Best case k=0: passenger 1 sits in seat 1; everyone sits correctly. For k=2 or k=3, the displacement loop either absorbs seat n (last is wrong) or closes at seat 1 (last is right). This ‚Äútwo outcomes per k‚Äù pattern again yields 1/2 overall.

    Divisibility of p^2 ‚àí 1 by 24 (for primes p ‚â• 5)

    Prove that for any prime p ‚â• 5, the number p^2 ‚àí 1 is divisible by 24.

    Added: Aug 30, 2025

    Clean proof via factors

    Factor the expression: p^2 ‚àí 1 = (p ‚àí 1)(p + 1). For primes p ‚â• 5, we have three consecutive integers p ‚àí 1, p, p + 1 with p odd and not divisible by 3.

    1) Divisible by 8.

    Since p is odd, both p ‚àí 1 and p + 1 are even. Among two consecutive even numbers, one is a multiple of 4 and the other is a multiple of 2. Hence (p ‚àí 1)(p + 1) is divisible by 4 √ó 2 = 8.

    2) Divisible by 3.

    Among any three consecutive integers exactly one is a multiple of 3. Since a prime p ‚â• 5 is not divisible by 3, either p ‚àí 1 or p + 1 is. Therefore (p ‚àí 1)(p + 1) is divisible by 3.

    3) Combine.

    We have 8 | (p^2 ‚àí 1) and 3 | (p^2 ‚àí 1), and gcd(8,3)=1. Hence lcm(8,3)=24 divides p^2 ‚àí 1. Thus for every prime p ‚â• 5, 24 | (p^2 ‚àí 1).

    Elegant modular view (one-liners)

    • mod 8: any odd m satisfies m^2 ‚â° 1 (mod 8), so p^2 ‚â° 1 (mod 8).
    • mod 3: a prime p ‚â• 5 is ¬±1 (mod 3), hence p^2 ‚â° 1 (mod 3).
    • By CRT, p^2 ‚â° 1 (mod 24) ‚ü∫ 24 | (p^2 ‚àí 1).

    Remark

    The argument actually shows: for any odd integer n not divisible by 3, n^2 ‚â° 1 (mod 24). Primes p ‚â• 5 are a special case.

    Goldbach Conjecture

    The famous unsolved statement that every even integer greater than 2 is the sum of two primes.

    Added: Aug 31, 2025
    Show Details ‚ñæ Hide Details ‚ñ¥

    The Story

    In 1742, German mathematician Christian Goldbach wrote to Leonhard Euler proposing that every integer greater than 2 is a sum of three primes. Euler replied with a sharper form ‚Äî now called the strong Goldbach Conjecture: Every even integer greater than 2 can be written as the sum of two primes.

    The conjecture is still unproven. Massive computations verify it for very large ranges, and deep analytic work (Hardy‚ÄìLittlewood circle method, etc.) has advanced our understanding. A landmark was Helfgott‚Äôs 2013 proof of the weak Goldbach (every odd integer ‚â•7 is the sum of three primes).

    Mathematical Statement

    For every even integer 2n > 2, there exist primes p, q such that 2n = p + q.

    Visualization: Every even number greater than 2 can be expressed as the sum of two primes.

    Examples

    4 = 2 + 2, 6 = 3 + 3, 8 = 3 + 5, 10 = 5 + 5 or 3 + 7, 28 = 5 + 23, ‚Ä¶

    Why it‚Äôs captivating

    The conjecture feels ‚Äúobviously true‚Äù from data and heuristics, yet resists proof. It sits at the crossroads of additive number theory, primes in arithmetic progressions, and the distribution of primes at large scales.

    Lemoine‚Äôs Conjecture (Also: Levy‚Äôs Conjecture)

    Every odd integer > 5 can be written as odd prime + even semiprime (that is, p + 2q).

    Added: Aug 31, 2025
    Show Details ‚ñæ Hide Details ‚ñ¥

    The Story

    Proposed in the late 19th century by French mathematician √âmile Lemoine (and later discussed by H. Levy), this conjecture mirrors Goldbach but for odd integers: write an odd number as one prime plus twice another prime. Despite its simple form and abundant computational evidence, it remains unproved.

    Mathematical Statement

    For every odd integer n > 5, there exist primes p, q (not necessarily distinct) such that n = p + 2q.

    Notes

    • An even semiprime here is exactly 2q with q prime.

    47 = 13 + 2√ó17, 37 = 2√ó5 + 27 (not valid), 41 = 11 + 2√ó15 (not valid), 41 = 5 + 2√ó18 (not valid), but 41 = 29 + 2√ó6 (not valid).     Why it‚Äôs interesting

    Like Goldbach, Lemoine‚Äôs conjecture blends the additive structure of the integers with the multiplicative structure of the primes. It is a natural ‚Äúodd-number analogue‚Äù whose truth would deepen our understanding of prime decompositions.

    Pirate‚Äôs Leftward Jump

    A pirate can jump onto any one of the 7 ships numbered 1 to 7. From then on, he may jump to any remaining ship; the ship he leaves sinks each time. The process continues until only one ship remains. A leftward jump means moving from a ship with a larger number to a smaller number. What is the probability that the pirate makes exactly one leftward jump?

    Added: Sep 1, 2025
    Show Solution ‚ñæ Hide Solution ‚ñ¥

    Recap

    The pirate‚Äôs journey corresponds to a random permutation of the 7 ships. A leftward jump occurs exactly when there is a descent in the permutation: Xk+1 < Xk.

    Key idea (splitting into two increasing blocks)

    If a permutation has exactly one descent, the numbers can be split into two sets X and Y:

    • All elements of X appear first, in increasing order.
    • Then comes the unique descent.
    • Finally, all elements of Y appear, also in increasing order.

    Example: X = {2,5,6}, Y = {1,3,4,7}. Sequence: 2,5,6 | 1,3,4,7. The only descent is at the cut.

    Counting valid splits

    Each non-trivial split of {1,‚Ä¶,7} into two sets X and Y gives exactly one valid permutation with one descent. Total subsets = 2‚Å∑ = 128. Exclude 8 invalid cases (where X or Y is empty or has all but one element). Hence valid = 120.

    Probability

    Total permutations = 7! = 5040. Favorable permutations = 120. Probability = 120 / 5040 = 1/42.

    Final Answer

    P(exactly one leftward jump) = 1/42.

    Two Friends Guess Each Other‚Äôs Coin Toss

    You and a friend each flip a fair coin. Without further communication, each of you must guess the result of the other‚Äôs coin. If both guesses are correct, your team earns 2 ‚Ç¨; if either guess is wrong, your team pays 1 ‚Ç¨. Should you be happy to play, or is the third person (the banker) in a better position?

    Added: Sep 2, 2025
    Show Solution ‚ñæ Hide Solution ‚ñ¥

    Recap

    Each round has two coin tosses: yours (C1) and your friend‚Äôs (C2). You each write down a guess for the other‚Äôs coin (N1 and N2). Payoffs: +2 ‚Ç¨ if both guesses are right, ‚àí1 ‚Ç¨ otherwise.

    Na√Øve random guessing

    If both players just guess randomly, then the chance both guesses match reality is (1/2) √ó (1/2) = 1/4. Expected payoff:

    EV = (1/4 √ó 2) + (3/4 √ó ‚àí1) = ‚àí1/4.

    In this case the banker wins on average; the players lose 0.25 ‚Ç¨ per game.

    A better strategy

    Suppose each player uses their own coin toss as their guess for the other‚Äôs coin (N1 = C1, N2 = C2). The four possible outcomes look like this:

    C1 C2 N1 (guess C2) N2 (guess C1) Result
    H H H H Both correct (+2 ‚Ç¨)
    T T T T Both correct (+2 ‚Ç¨)
    H T H T Both wrong (‚àí1 ‚Ç¨)
    T H T H Both wrong (‚àí1 ‚Ç¨)

    So probability of winning is 1/2 (coins match). Expected payoff:

    EV = (1/2 √ó 2) + (1/2 √ó ‚àí1) = +1/2.

    Conclusion

    With random guesses, the banker profits. With the ‚Äúcopy your own toss‚Äù strategy, your team‚Äôs expected gain is +0.5 ‚Ç¨ per round. So you definitely prefer being on the players‚Äô team!

    Drunk Ant on a Cube

    An ant starts at one corner of a cube and moves randomly along edges, one step at a time. What is the expected number of edges the ant travels before reaching the opposite corner?

    Added: Sep 05, 2025
    Show Solution ‚ñæ Hide Solution ‚ñ¥

    Step 1: Grouping the states

    Vertices are grouped by their Hamming distance to the target corner Sf:

    • S0: start vertex (distance 3)
    • Si1: vertices at distance 2
    • Si2: vertices at distance 1
    • Sf: target vertex (distance 0)

    All vertices in the same group are symmetric, so expectations are identical within a group.

    Step 2: Transition probabilities

    • From S0: always ‚Üí Si1
    • From Si1: 2/3 ‚Üí Si2, 1/3 ‚Üí S0
    • From Si2: 1/3 ‚Üí Sf, 2/3 ‚Üí Si1
    • From Sf: absorbing, stay at Sf

    Step 3: First-step equations

    Let EX = expected steps to reach Sf from state X. In particular: ESf = 0.

    • ES0 = 1 + ESi1
    • ESi2 = 1 + (1/3)¬∑ESf + (2/3)¬∑ESi1 = 1 + (2/3)¬∑ESi1
    • ESi1 = 1 + (2/3)¬∑ESi2 + (1/3)¬∑ES0

    Step 4: Solve the system

    From ES0 = 1 + ESi1, substitute into ESi1 equation:

    ESi1 = 1 + (2/3)¬∑ESi2 + (1/3)(1 + ESi1)

    (2/3)¬∑ESi1 = 4/3 + (2/3)¬∑ESi2 ‚áí ESi1 = 2 + ESi2

    Now use ESi2 = 1 + (2/3)¬∑ESi1:

    ESi2 = 1 + (2/3)(2 + ESi2) = 7/3 + (2/3)¬∑ESi2

    (1/3)¬∑ESi2 = 7/3 ‚áí ESi2 = 7

    ESi1 = 2 + 7 = 9

    ES0 = 1 + 9 = 10

    Final Answer

    The expected number of steps the ant takes to reach the opposite corner is 10.
