  • Airplane Seating (Lost Boarding Pass)

    There are n passengers and n seats. Passenger 1 sits randomly; each later passenger takes their own seat if free, otherwise picks randomly. What’s the probability that the last passenger sits in their own seat? More generally, what’s the probability that the k-th passenger from the end finds their seat vacant?

    Added: Aug 30, 2025
    Show Solution ▾ Hide Solution ▴

    Recap

    P(A) = (number of outcomes where A happens) / (total number of outcomes). Here A is “the last passenger sits in the proper seat.”

    Key idea (two special seats)

    Track only seat 1 (for passenger 1) and seat n (for the last passenger). Passenger 1 chooses a seat. Anyone whose seat is taken picks a random free seat; think of a “wrong-seat token” that jumps each time that happens. The process stops the first time the chain hits either seat 1 or seat n:

    • If the chain hits seat 1 first, seat n was never taken ⇒ the last passenger sits correctly.
    • If it hits seat n first, the last passenger’s seat is already occupied ⇒ they cannot sit correctly.

    Counting by how many sit wrong (k-view)

    Let k be the number of people who end up in the wrong seat. We never have k = 1. For each admissible k (0, 2, 3, …, n) there are two symmetric ways the chain can close: at seat 1 or at seat n. Exactly half of those cases give the last passenger their correct seat.

    Result for the last passenger

    P(last sits correctly) = 1/2 for every n ≥ 2. Symmetry between the two special seats makes them equally likely to be the first one hit.

    Generalization: k-th from last

    Consider passenger number n − k + 1 (the k-th from the end). Track the special set S = {1, n−k+1, n−k+2, …, n} of size k+1. Just before that passenger boards, the wrong-seat token is equally likely to be on any seat in S. Their seat is free exactly when the token is on one of the other k seats. Hence: P(k-th from last finds own seat free) = k / (k + 1).

    Checks: k=1 → 1/2 (the last passenger), k=2 → 2/3, k=3 → 3/4, independent of n (for n ≥ k+1).

    Alternative intuition via cases

    Best case k=0: passenger 1 sits in seat 1; everyone sits correctly. For k=2 or k=3, the displacement loop either absorbs seat n (last is wrong) or closes at seat 1 (last is right). This “two outcomes per k” pattern again yields 1/2 overall.

  • Divisibility of p^2 − 1 by 24 (for primes p ≥ 5)

    Prove that for any prime p ≥ 5, the number p^2 − 1 is divisible by 24.

    Added: Aug 30, 2025
    Show Solution ▾ Hide Solution ▴

    Goal

    Show that 24 | (p^2 − 1) for any prime p ≥ 5. Since 24 = 3 × 8 and gcd(3,8)=1, it suffices to prove 3 | (p^2 − 1) and 8 | (p^2 − 1).

    Step 1 — Divisible by 3

    A prime p ≥ 5 is not divisible by 3, so p ≡ 1 or p ≡ 2 ≡ −1 (mod 3). In either case, p^2 ≡ 1 (mod 3) ⇒ p^2 − 1 ≡ 0 (mod 3).

    Step 2 — Divisible by 8

    Any prime p ≥ 5 is odd: write p = 2k + 1. Then p^2 − 1 = (2k + 1)^2 − 1 = 4k(k + 1). Since k and k+1 are consecutive, one of them is even; hence k(k+1) is even and 4 · k(k+1) is a multiple of 8. Therefore 8 | (p^2 − 1).

    Conclusion

    We have 3 | (p^2 − 1) and 8 | (p^2 − 1) with gcd(3,8)=1. By the divisibility product rule, 24 | (p^2 − 1).

    Alternative one-liner (mod 6 / mod 24 view)

    For primes p ≥ 5, p ≡ ±1 (mod 6). Checking residues mod 24 (or using the facts above), any odd p gives p^2 ≡ 1 (mod 8), and p ≡ ±1 (mod 3) gives p^2 ≡ 1 (mod 3), hence p^2 ≡ 1 (mod 24).

    Critical ideas to remember

    • If A|C and B|C with gcd(A,B)=1, then AB|C.
    • For any odd integer m, m^2 ≡ 1 (mod 8).
    • For primes p ≥ 5, p ≡ ±1 (mod 3) ⇒ p^2 ≡ 1 (mod 3).
