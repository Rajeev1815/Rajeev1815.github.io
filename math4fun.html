Airplane Seating (Lost Boarding Pass)

There are n passengers and n seats. Passenger 1 (who lost the pass) takes a seat uniformly at random. Every later passenger takes their own seat if it‚Äôs free; otherwise they pick a random free seat. What is the probability the last passenger sits in their own seat? More generally, what is the probability that the kth passenger from the end finds their seat vacant?

Show Solution

1) Recap: probability

\(P(\text{event A})=\dfrac{\text{# outcomes where A happens}}{\text{# all possible outcomes}}\). Here, event A is ‚Äúthe last passenger sits in the proper seat.‚Äù

2) Structure of what happens

Focus on who is forced to choose randomly. Passenger 1 starts the process by sitting in some seat. If that seat happens to be theirs (seat 1), the process ends immediately: everyone‚Äîincluding the last passenger‚Äîsits correctly. If not, suppose passenger 1 sits in seat j. Then when passenger j boards they find their seat taken and must choose randomly; this may displace someone else, and so on. The ‚Äúwrong-seat token‚Äù moves forward each time a displaced passenger chooses at random.

This creates a chain (or loop) of displaced people. Crucially, the chain ends the first time it hits either seat 1 (passenger 1‚Äôs proper seat) or seat n (the last passenger‚Äôs proper seat):

  • If the chain hits seat 1 first, the last passenger‚Äôs seat has stayed free all along, so the last passenger sits correctly.
  • If the chain hits seat n first, the last passenger‚Äôs seat is occupied before they board, so they cannot sit correctly.

3) Counting by ‚Äúhow many sit wrong‚Äù (your \(k\)-view)

Let \(k\) be the number of people who ultimately sit in the wrong seat. We never have \(k=1\) (one person alone can‚Äôt be wrong), and \(k\) can be \(0,2,3,\dots,n\).

  • Best case \(k=0\): passenger 1 accidentally chooses seat 1. Everyone sits correctly; last passenger succeeds.
  • Case \(k=2\): two people form the loop of wrong seats. (i) passenger 1 sits in seat n ‚Üí last passenger eventually gets seat 1 ‚Üí last is wrong. (ii) passenger 1 sits in some seat \(j\neq 1,n\) and the displaced person eventually hits seat 1 ‚Üí loop closes at 1 ‚Üí last is correct.
  • Case \(k=3\): three people form the loop. There are again two flavors: one where the loop absorbs seat n (last becomes wrong), and one where it closes at seat 1 (last is correct).

In general, for each admissible \(k\in\{0,2,3,\dots,n\}\) there are two symmetric ways the displaced chain can terminate: at seat 1 or at seat n. Half of these \(k\)-scenarios lead to the last passenger being correct, and half lead to being incorrect.

4) Symmetry punchline ‚Üí \( \mathbf{1/2} \)

The random process only cares about which of the two special seats \((1 \text{ or } n)\) is hit first by the chain of displacements. By symmetry, these two are equally likely. Therefore \[ \Pr(\text{last passenger sits correctly})=\tfrac{1}{2} \qquad\text{for all } n\ge 2. \]

5) Generalization: \(k\)th from last

Let‚Äôs look at the passenger who is \(k\)th from the end, i.e., passenger \(n-k+1\). Track the \((k+1)\) ‚Äúspecial‚Äù seats \[ S=\{1,\, n-k+1,\, n-k+2,\,\dots,\, n\}. \] Before any of these special seats is finally fixed, the ‚Äúwrong-seat token‚Äù is equally likely to be on any of the currently free seats in \(S\). When passenger \(n-k+1\) arrives, the token is uniformly on one of the \((k+1)\) seats in \(S\); their own seat is vacant exactly when the token is on one of the other \(k\) seats. Hence \[ \Pr\big(\text{\(k\)th from last finds their seat free}\big)=\frac{k}{k+1}. \]

Checks: \(k=1\Rightarrow 1/2\) (the last passenger), \(k=2\Rightarrow 2/3\), \(k=3\Rightarrow 3/4\), etc., independent of \(n\) (as long as \(n\ge k+1\)).
