  • Airplane Seating (Lost Boarding Pass)

    There are n passengers and n seats. Passenger 1 lost the pass and sits in a random seat. Each later passenger takes their own seat if free; otherwise they choose a random free seat. What is the probability the last passenger sits in their own seat? More generally, what is the probability the k-th passenger from the end finds their seat vacant?

    Recap

    P(A) = (number of outcomes where A happens) / (total number of outcomes).

    Key idea

    Track only the two ‚Äúspecial‚Äù seats: seat 1 (for passenger 1) and seat n (for the last passenger). When someone‚Äôs proper seat is taken, they pick a random free seat and the ‚Äúwrong-seat token‚Äù jumps. The process stops the first time the chain hits seat 1 or seat n.

    • If the chain hits seat 1 first, seat n was never taken; the last passenger sits correctly.
    • If it hits seat n first, the last passenger‚Äôs seat is occupied; they cannot sit correctly.

    Counting by how many sit wrong (k-view)

    Let k be the number of people who end up in the wrong seat. We can have k = 0, 2, 3, ‚Ä¶, n (never 1). For each admissible k there are two symmetric ways the chain can close: at seat 1 or at seat n. Exactly half of those cases give the last passenger their correct seat.

    Result for the last passenger

    P(last sits correctly) = 1/2 for every n ‚â• 2.

    Generalization

    Consider the passenger who is k-th from the end (number n ‚àí k + 1). Track the set of special seats S = {1, n‚àík+1, n‚àík+2, ‚Ä¶, n} (size k+1). Just before that passenger boards, the wrong-seat token is equally likely to be on any seat in S. Their own seat is free exactly when the token is on one of the other k seats.

    P(k-th from last finds own seat free) = k / (k + 1).

    Checks: k=1 ‚Üí 1/2 (last passenger), k=2 ‚Üí 2/3, k=3 ‚Üí 3/4, etc., independent of n (for n ‚â• k+1).
